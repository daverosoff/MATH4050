Proof.

Proof of (3). For each \(n \in \N\text{,}\) let
\begin{equation*} f_n \colon A_n \to \N \end{equation*}
be a bijection. Choose \(x \in \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n\) and set \(n_x = \min \{ k \in \N : x \in A_k \}\text{.}\) Now define
\begin{equation*} f \colon \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n \to \N \times \N \end{equation*}
by
\begin{equation*} f(x) = 2^{n_x} 3^{f_{n_x}(x)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Let us show that \(f\) is injective. So suppose that \(f(x) = f(y)\) for some \(x, y \in \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n\text{.}\) Then
\begin{equation*} 2^{n_x} 3^{f_{n_x}(x)} = 2^{n_y} 3^{f_{n_y}(y)}. \end{equation*}
Using the unique factorization of positive integers into primes, we have \(n_x = n_y\) and \(f_{n_x}(x) = f_{n_y}(y)\text{,}\) and \(f_{n_x}(x) = f_{n_x}(y)\text{.}\) But \(f_{n_x}\) is injective. Thus \(x = y\) and \(f\) is injective.
